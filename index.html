08What this means for Globe From Home

Heat kills nearly 550,000 people every year. It wipes out over a trillion dollars in productivity. It closes tens of thousands of schools, cripples agriculture, and hits the poorest communities hardest. By 2050, over a million people could die annually and 970 cities will regularly exceed 35 degrees.

But structured interventions work. Ahmedabad's Heat Action Plan reduced heat stroke deaths from 65 to 3 per year and saves an estimated 1,190 lives annually. Cool roofs reduce indoor temperatures by around 3 degrees C in slum housing. These are documented, peer-reviewed, and ready to scale.

What is missing is the layer that activates everything else: community education. Cooling centres sit empty if people do not know they exist. Warnings fail if communities do not understand what to do. Cool roofs do not get adopted if households do not see the value. Education is the multiplier. That is what Globe From Home delivers through live-streamed, empathy-driven experiences that connect learners to the realities of heat and move them to action.

About the data behind this dashboard

We built this dashboard using only peer-reviewed research, official UN and government agency reports, and data from recognised global partnerships. No blog posts, no opinion pieces, no unverified claims. Here is a quick guide to the key sources and why we picked each one.

For mortality data, we draw primarily from two studies. The ISGlobal study published in Nature Medicine (2025) covers heat deaths across 654 regions in 32 European countries over three consecutive summers. We chose it because it is the most rigorous, most current study of its kind and gives us year-on-year trends rather than a single snapshot. The headline global figure of 546,000 annual deaths comes from the Lancet Countdown 2025 report, which is the most widely referenced annual tracker of climate and health globally.

For economic impact, we rely on Zhao et al. in Nature (2024) because it is the first study to model not just direct productivity loss but also the ripple effects through global supply chains. Most economic estimates miss these indirect costs, which means they understate the real damage. We also use ILO data for worker-specific figures and the US Joint Economic Committee (2023) for American context, because many of our target funders are US-based and respond to data from their own backyard.

For the inequality angle, Callahan and Mankin in Science Advances (2022) is our primary source. It demonstrates that the poorest regions lose over twice as much GDP per capita from heat as the richest. This is the data point that connects our work to the climate justice narrative that equity-focused funders prioritise.

For proof that interventions work, the Ahmedabad Heat Action Plan is our anchor case study, documented in Hess et al. (2018) and AAQR (2022). It remains the most rigorously evaluated heat action plan in the developing world, with clear before-and-after mortality data.

Reading the numbers

A few things to keep in mind when looking at the data on this dashboard.

Reported vs actual deaths. Throughout this dashboard you will see two types of death figures. "Reported" or "official" numbers are what appears on death certificates, where a doctor has specifically coded heat as the cause. "Estimated actual" or "excess mortality" numbers come from a different method: researchers look at how many people died during a heat event compared to how many would normally die during that same period, and the difference is the estimated heat toll. The second method captures all the cardiac arrests, kidney failures, and respiratory collapses that heat caused but that were not coded as heat deaths. This is why estimated figures are always significantly higher than official ones.

Economic projections. The cost figures on this dashboard range from $1.09 trillion today to $24.7 trillion by 2100. That wide range reflects different emissions scenarios. We show the spectrum rather than a single number so readers can see both the trajectory we are on and what could change with intervention. Where a single figure is shown, we have used the more conservative estimate.

The Ahmedabad numbers. We reference two different figures for Ahmedabad: heat stroke deaths (which dropped from 65 to 3) and total lives saved (~1,190 per year). These come from different analyses. The heat stroke count tracks specific diagnosed cases at hospitals. The 1,190 figure comes from a broader epidemiological study comparing all-cause excess mortality during heat season before and after the plan. Both point to the same conclusion.

How current is this data?

We have used the most recent published data available for every metric as of March 2026. Here is what that means in practice.

The European mortality figures (2022 to 2024) come from a study published in September 2025 covering through summer 2024. Summer 2025 data will likely appear in late 2026. The global 546,000 figure is from Lancet Countdown 2025, which updated the previous estimate of 489,000 that covered 2000 to 2019.

The US data runs through 2023 for reported deaths (JAMA 2024) and through 2020 for excess mortality estimates (Yale SPH 2025). The Ahmedabad case study has detailed published data through 2018, though the programme itself continues to operate and update annually.

For future updates, the Lancet Countdown publishes new figures every October or November. The UNEP Frontiers report updates regularly. Both are worth checking before any major funder presentation. If you need city-level data more recent than what is published, the Ahmedabad Municipal Corporation and the C40 Cool Cities Accelerator network are the best sources to reach out to directly.

What works: evidence-backed solutions
Each intervention below is grounded in peer-reviewed research and real-world implementation. Organised around Globe From Home's workshop programme, drawing on Project Drawdown, Ahmedabad HAP data, and field evidence from partner cities.
01

Personal heat response (first line of defence)

What it is
Training individuals to recognise heat illness symptoms, take protective action (hydration, shade-seeking, modified work schedules), administer basic first aid for heat stroke, and share knowledge with family and community. This is the "first line of defence" that Rajeev identified as foundational to the programme.
Evidence of effectiveness
Heat stroke deaths: 65 to 3
Ahmedabad's Heat Action Plan, which combines early warnings with community education and health worker training, reduced annual heat stroke deaths from 65 (2010) to just 3 (2018).
Hess et al. 2018, Environmental Health Perspectives
Up to 80% fatality if untreated
Heat stroke is a medical emergency. Mortality reaches 80% without prompt cooling and treatment. Basic first response knowledge (recognising symptoms, rapid cooling, calling for help) is the difference between life and death.
Sheraton et al. 2023, BJA Education
70%+ awareness post-outreach
Ahmedabad's community campaigns raised heat illness awareness from under 30% to above 70% in targeted communities.
Knowlton et al. 2014, IIPH-G / Ahmedabad HAP
Expected outcomes from Globe From Home workshops
25 to 40% of participants adopt 3+ protective behaviours within 90 days. Each trained person shares knowledge with an average of 12 additional family and community members (4 household + 8 community), creating a cascade multiplier effect.
02

Cool roofs

What it is
Applying solar-reflective paint or materials to rooftops, particularly in low-income and slum housing. Reflects sunlight rather than absorbing it, reducing indoor temperatures without electricity or air conditioning. One of the most cost-effective heat interventions available.
Evidence of effectiveness
~3 degrees C indoor reduction
Measured in Ahmedabad slum housing after solar reflective white paint application. Pilot covered 3,000+ low-income homes through Mahila Housing Trust.
Vellingiri et al. 2020, Indian J Occup Environ Med
3 to 6 degrees C in field reports
Broader field reports from 20,000+ homes treated with cool roof technology across Ahmedabad slums show indoor reductions of 3 to 6 degrees C depending on roof material.
Mahila Housing Trust / NRDC field data 2024
20 to 40% cooling energy savings
Cool roofs reduce indoor temperatures enough to significantly cut air conditioning demand. In buildings with AC, this translates to measurable energy and emissions savings.
NRDC / Ahmedabad Heat Action Plan 2016
Expected outcomes
Target: 80 m2 of cool roof per trained champion. At a deployment rate of 65% (based on Ahmedabad's community health worker activation rate of 60 to 70%), each cohort of 500 champions creates 26,000 m2 of cool roof surface and avoids approximately 65 tonnes of CO2 per year.
03

Urban gardens and green corridors

What it is
Establishing community gardens, tree canopy, and green corridors in urban heat islands. Vegetation cools through evapotranspiration and shade, while also providing food security, biodiversity, air quality, and community gathering benefits.
Evidence of effectiveness
1.2 degrees C per 10% canopy increase
Ambient temperature reduction for every 10% increase in urban tree canopy coverage.
Ziter et al. 2019, PNAS
2 degrees C corridor cooling
Medellin's 36 green corridors reduced surrounding temperatures by approximately 2 degrees C while connecting fragmented urban ecosystems.
C40 Cities / Medellin Green Corridors programme data
Most funded heat intervention globally
In a review of over 200 World Bank urban projects, green features (parks, trees, green spaces) were the single most common type of heat intervention, appearing in 48% of all heat-related projects. This makes greening the most bankable and scalable heat solution.
World Bank, Combating Heat in Cities 2024
Expected outcomes
Target: 22 community garden sites per pilot city. Expected 2.1 degrees C ambient cooling in surrounding area. Co-benefits include local food production, improved air quality, and community social cohesion.
04

School heat mapping and adaptation

What it is
Mapping heat exposure in and around schools to identify the hottest classrooms, routes, and outdoor areas. Using this data to drive adaptations: shade structures, schedule changes, hydration stations, and heat-aware teaching practices. Directly addresses the 33,000 school closures seen in Bangladesh and the 21% learning outcome decline documented in heat-exposed students.
Evidence of effectiveness
Cumulative learning loss from heat
Each additional hot school day (above 32 degrees C) measurably reduces test scores. The effect compounds over the school year and disproportionately affects students without access to cooling.
Park et al. 2020, Nature Human Behaviour
559 million children exposed
To high heatwave frequency globally. Many in the Global South walk to school in extreme heat without shade or cooling infrastructure.
UNICEF, The Coldest Year 2022
242 million students disrupted
In 85 countries during 2024 alone. Heatwaves were the most common climate hazard causing school closures, affecting 118 million students in April alone.
UNICEF, Learning Interrupted 2025
Expected outcomes
Target: 35 schools per pilot city mapped and adapted. Expected 15 to 17% attendance improvement during heat season. Heat mapping data becomes a reusable asset for city planners and education departments.
05

Solar-powered fans for schools

What it is
Installing solar-powered ventilation and cooling fans in classrooms. Provides immediate thermal relief without grid dependency or electricity cost, making it sustainable for schools in off-grid or energy-poor areas. Pairs with cool roofs and shade structures for compound cooling effect.
Evidence of effectiveness
Global cooling demand tripling by 2050
Energy demand for space cooling is projected to triple by mid-century, mostly in developing countries. Solar-powered alternatives bypass grid dependency and recurring energy costs entirely.
IEA, The Future of Cooling 2018
Air-conditioned schools offset 80% of heat's learning impact
Park et al. found that schools with air conditioning experienced roughly one-fifth of the learning loss compared to schools without any cooling. Even basic ventilation (fans, airflow) provides meaningful improvement.
Park et al. 2020, Nature Human Behaviour
Expected outcomes
Target: 35 schools equipped per pilot city. Combined with cool roofs and schedule adaptation, expected to maintain classroom temperatures within tolerable learning range during 90%+ of school hours in heat season.
06

Advocacy campaigns

What it is
Training community leaders, parents, teachers, and local officials to advocate for heat-protective policies: workplace temperature limits, school schedule adjustments, public cooling infrastructure, and heat action plan adoption. This is how individual awareness becomes systemic change.
Evidence of effectiveness
23 states, 130+ cities
Following Ahmedabad's model, India's NDMA is now working with 23 heat-prone states and over 130 cities and districts to develop and implement heat action plans. Community demand and political pressure drove this national scale-up.
Padmanaban 2021, Eos / AGU; NRDC / IIPHG India programme
33 founding cities
C40 launched the Cool Cities Accelerator in November 2025 with 33 founding cities and backing from Rockefeller Foundation. Growing political will means advocacy has receptive audiences.
C40 Cool Cities Accelerator 2025
Most heat laws are too general
In most countries, existing workplace heat provisions fail to address the specific dangers workers face. Targeted advocacy for enforceable temperature limits, mandatory rest periods, and hydration access is the gap.
ILO, Heat at Work 2024
Expected outcomes
Target: 600 community advocates trained per pilot city. Expected to drive at least 1 local policy change per city within 12 months (workplace breaks, school schedule adjustments, or public cooling infrastructure). 28% reduction in heat symptoms in communities with active advocacy programmes.

1.5°C is a survival threshold
For small island developing states, climate change is not a future risk but a present emergency. Rising seas, intensifying heat, and coral bleaching threaten entire national economies and habitability.
IPCC AR6 WGII, Chapter 15: Small Islands
Compound vulnerability
Sea level rise + heat + storms
Island communities face overlapping climate risks with very limited infrastructure, healthcare capacity, and fiscal space to respond. Heat compounds every other stressor.
UNEP, Global Adaptation Gap Report 2024
Tourism-dependent economies
30 to 80% of GDP from tourism
Heat and climate impacts directly threaten the economic engine of most small island nations. Building community resilience protects both lives and livelihoods.
World Bank, SIDS Development Data
02Target regions
Focus on island nations with strong government engagement on climate, existing international partnerships, and scalable school systems.
🏝️

Fiji

COP23 presidency. Strong climate policy leadership. 900,000 population with centralised education system. Existing partnerships with UNDP and Pacific Islands Forum.
🏝️

Maldives

Global symbol of climate vulnerability. Extreme heat and humidity. 500,000 population. Government actively seeking climate adaptation partnerships.
🏝️

Tuvalu

One of the most climate-vulnerable nations on earth. 11,000 population allows for whole-nation pilot. Digital statehood initiative provides unique partnership opportunity.
03Scale projections

Projected reach over 3 years

Island model: smaller populations but whole-nation coverage possible. High visibility for global advocacy.
Year 1
1,500 learners across 1 nation
Single-country deep pilot (likely Fiji). Full school system integration and community training.
Year 2
5,000 learners across 3 nations
Expansion to Maldives and Tuvalu. Adaptation of content for island-specific heat and humidity contexts.
Year 3
15,000 learners across 6 nations
Pacific and Indian Ocean coverage. Model presented at UNFCCC and Pacific Islands Forum as replicable framework.
